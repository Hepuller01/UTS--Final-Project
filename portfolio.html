Joining the Rover Scouts and climbing a mountain for the first time helped me grow in different ways. According to Erikson's theory, stepping into new roles allowed me to explore who I am beyond my past experiences. Interacting with different grade levels taught me important social skills, as I observed and learned from others, following Bandura's Social Learning Theory. Overall, these experiences helped me understand myself better.
Christmas Eve gatherings help me grow because they give me chances to connect with my family and learn more about myself. According to Erikson's theory, spending time with my relatives strengthens my sense of belonging and helps me build relationships, which is important for shaping my identity. Vygotsky's theory shows me how these family traditions, like sharing meals and playing games, shape my understanding of culture and influence my thinking. Through Bandura's theory, I learn by observing my relatives' behavior, which helps me understand different roles and how to act in social situations. Piaget's theory explains how these events also help me manage my emotions and think about how I feel in different moments. As I interact with my family, I become more aware of who I am and how I relate to others. These experiences make me better at communicating, empathizing, and adapting in different social situations. Overall, Christmas Eve helps me grow emotionally, socially, and culturally, giving me a deeper understanding of myself.
In this experience, I can relate to Albert Bandura's Social Cognitive Theory. At first, I had very low self-confidence and didn't believe in my ability to succeed in the pageant. However, the support I received from my family, teachers, and classmates helped boost my confidence through social persuasion. According to Bandura, seeing others believe in me made me start believing in myself. As I continued despite my fears, I gained personal mastery experiences, which helped me build a stronger belief in my abilities. Over time, this process allowed me to overcome my doubts and feel more confident in who I was.
When I joined the School Press in Grade 12, it was a big step for me because I had doubts about my abilities after stopping in high school. According to Carl Rogers' Self-Concept Theory, I had a negative self-concept at the time, believing I wasn’t capable. However, a classmate recommended me, and despite my hesitation, I decided to take the risk, reflecting the idea of self-actualization, where I pushed beyond my comfort zone to realize my potential. Before the results came out, I deleted my Facebook and Messenger to avoid seeing any negative feedback, which showed my fear of disappointment, aligning with the concept of self-protection. When I woke up and saw a message telling me I made it, it boosted my self-efficacy (a belief in my ability to succeed), which is part of Albert Bandura's Social Cognitive Theory. Making it to the top 7 and eventually winning third place proved to me that I was capable of more than I thought. This experience taught me that personal growth often comes from stepping outside your comfort zone and taking risks, even when you're unsure of the outcome.
Now that I’ve graduated to Grade 12, I’ve been reflecting on how far I’ve come in understanding who I am. Erik Erikson’s theory of Identity vs. Role Confusion really fits my experience over the past few years. My mom has always been there for me, offering support and encouragement that helped me believe in myself, even during tough times. My friends have shown me different perspectives, helping me explore what I truly value and want in life. Teachers played a big role, too, pushing me to exceed my limits and boosting my confidence in my abilities. Thanks to all their guidance, I feel like I’ve gained a better sense of who I am and what I want to become. It’s been a journey of self-discovery, and although I still have much to learn, I feel ready for the next chapter.
When my friends came over to celebrate my birthday, even though we couldn't hang out earlier, it really made me feel good about myself.
